Part I:

Simple Patterns and Explanations

Powers of 2

For each row in Pascal’s Triangle, the sum of all the numbers in the row is equal to 2^(row number).

1 = 1

1 + 1 = 2

1 + 2 + 1 = 4

1 + 3 + 3 + 1 = 8

This happens because every number in a row is added to two other numbers when you move to the next row, thereby ‘doubling’ itself.

Powers of 11

If each number of a row is used to represent a place in base 10, the total of each row is 11^(row number).

1*(100) = 1 = 110

1*(101) +1*(100) = 11 = 111

1*(102) + 2*(101) + 1*(100) = 121 = 112

1*(103) + 3*(102) + 3*(101) + 1*(100) = 1331 = 113

This happens because the method for multiplying by eleven is the same as the method for finding a new row in Pascal’s Triangle:

121 * 11 or 121 -> new row

Hockey Sticks

If you start at any ‘1’ on Pascal’s Triangle and continue down a diagonal, you can find the sum of that diagonal (so far) by checking the next number in the opposite diagonal. This description probably doesn’t make much sense, so I’ve included a picture.

In this picture, you can see that the sum of any diagonal is shown by the colored box.

1 + 3 + 6 = 10

1 + 5 + 15 + 35 + 70 = 126

1 + 9 = 10

If you look at the red ‘stick’, for example, you can see that each number is moved to the diagonal below the red boxes when you move to a new row. In other words, it keeps a running total:

1 = 1

1 + 5 = 6

6 + 15 = 21

21 + 35 = 56

56 + 70 = 126

Sierpinski’s Triangle

If you highlight the odd numbers in Pascal’s Triangle, you get a Sierpinski Triangle.

Prime Numbers[a]

For any row of Pascal’s triangle where the first number is prime- remember, the 1 is the 0th number- all other numbers in the row will be multiples of it (excluding ones).

Flower Pattern

For any number in Pascal’s Triangle, the adjacent numbers form two groups whose products are equal. Examples in this image:

1 * 3 * 4  =  1 * 2 * 6

1 * 5 * 21  =  1 * 7 * 15

5 * 20 * 21  =  6 * 10 * 35
